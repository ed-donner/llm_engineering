# The Lean Startup — Eric Ries

## Overview

The Lean Startup is Eric Ries's methodology for building companies under conditions of extreme uncertainty. It applies lean manufacturing principles — originally developed by Toyota — to the startup context. The central insight is that most startups fail not because of bad execution, but because they execute the wrong plan. The solution is to replace big planning cycles with fast, iterative experiments that test assumptions before significant resources are committed.

The methodology is applicable not just to two-person startups but to any organisation, team, or individual trying to innovate under uncertainty.

## The Build-Measure-Learn Feedback Loop

The fundamental unit of Lean Startup activity is the Build-Measure-Learn loop. The goal is to minimise the total time through this loop:

1. **Build** — create a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) that tests your most important assumption.
2. **Measure** — use actionable metrics to determine what the data reveals about customer behaviour.
3. **Learn** — decide whether to persevere with your hypothesis or pivot to a new one.

Every activity a startup does should be directed toward completing this loop as quickly and cheaply as possible. Speed matters because every untested assumption is a liability.

## Validated Learning

Ries argues that the true measure of startup progress is *validated learning*: learning something true about customers, tested through real experiments. This is not the same as learning through market research, surveys, or expert opinion — it must come from empirical data produced by real customer interactions.

Validated learning is the answer to the question: "What did we actually learn this week?" A startup that burned $1M learning nothing has failed, even if it looks busy. A startup that learned one critical truth with $50K has succeeded at its core task.

## The Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

The MVP is not the smallest possible version of the product — it is the fastest way to get through the Build-Measure-Learn loop with the least effort. Its purpose is to test a specific leap-of-faith assumption.

Types of MVPs:
- **Concierge MVP**: manually deliver a service to a small number of customers to validate demand before automating. (Airbnb founders photographing listings themselves.)
- **Wizard of Oz MVP**: the product appears to work automatically, but a human is operating it behind the scenes.
- **Landing page MVP**: a simple page describing the product. Measure sign-ups. Dropbox used a video explaining the product before building it.

The danger: *vanity MVPs* — building what you assume the customer wants without testing the core assumption.

## Leap-of-Faith Assumptions

Every startup business plan rests on two fundamental assumptions:
1. **Value hypothesis**: does the product actually create value for customers? Does it solve the problem you think it does?
2. **Growth hypothesis**: how will new customers discover the product? Is the growth engine sustainable?

These are the riskiest parts of any plan. They should be tested first, not last. The job of the startup is to design experiments that test these assumptions cheaply and quickly.

## Innovation Accounting

Traditional accounting metrics (revenue, profit) are not useful for early-stage startups because they tell you where you've been, not whether you're learning. Ries introduces *innovation accounting* as a way to measure progress objectively.

Three steps:
1. Use an MVP to establish a baseline on key metrics.
2. Tune the engine — improve metrics through product experiments.
3. Make a pivot-or-persevere decision based on whether progress is satisfactory.

A startup that is not improving its metrics despite experiments is accumulating waste. Time to pivot.

## Vanity Metrics vs. Actionable Metrics

Vanity metrics: numbers that look good on paper but don't drive decisions. Total page views, total registered users, total downloads. These grow over time simply because the company exists. They feel good but reveal nothing about whether the product is working.

Actionable metrics: metrics tied to specific actions. Conversion rate from visitor to trial, cohort retention rates, revenue per customer. These can be improved through deliberate product changes and reveal whether the product is actually working.

The standard: if a metric can't change your behaviour, it's vanity.

## Three Engines of Growth

Ries identifies three sustainable engines of growth for startups:

1. **The Sticky Engine**: growth through customer retention. Customers who stay use the product long-term. Measure: churn rate. If acquisition rate exceeds churn rate, the company grows. Example: SaaS businesses.
2. **The Viral Engine**: growth through customer referrals built into the product. Measure: viral coefficient (how many new users each existing user generates). A viral coefficient above 1 means exponential growth. Example: Hotmail's "Get your free email at Hotmail" in every email footer.
3. **The Paid Engine**: growth through paid acquisition. Works when Customer Lifetime Value (CLV) exceeds Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC). Scale by reducing CAC or increasing CLV.

Each engine has its own set of metrics. A startup should focus on one engine at a time.

## The Pivot

A pivot is a structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, strategy, and engine of growth. It is not failure — it is a recognition that the current path is not working, combined with the discipline to change based on evidence.

Types of pivots:
- **Zoom-in pivot**: what was a feature becomes the whole product.
- **Zoom-out pivot**: what was the product becomes a feature of a larger product.
- **Customer segment pivot**: same product, different customer.
- **Customer need pivot**: the problem being solved turns out to be less important than a different problem the same customer has.
- **Platform pivot**: from application to platform.
- **Business architecture pivot**: high margin / low volume (enterprise) to low margin / high volume (consumer), or vice versa.
- **Technology pivot**: same solution, different technology.

The runway of a startup is not just the cash it has — it is the number of pivots it can still make. Startups should optimise for learning fast, not spending slowly, because a pivot that costs $50K is better than burning $2M validating the wrong assumption.

## Five Whys (Root Cause Analysis)

When something goes wrong, ask "Why?" five times in succession. Each answer becomes the basis for the next question. The fifth answer usually reveals a deeper systemic or process failure, not an individual mistake. This drives proportional investment in fixing the root cause, not the symptom.

## Key Quotes

- "The only way to win is to learn faster than anyone else."
- "We must learn what customers really want, not what they say they want, or what we think they should want."
- "A startup is a human institution designed to create a new product or service under conditions of extreme uncertainty."
- "Startup productivity is not about cranking out more widgets or writing more code. It's about aligning our efforts with a business and product that are working to create value and drive growth."
- "The question is not 'Can this product be built?' Nearly anything can be built today. The real question is 'Should this product be built?'"
