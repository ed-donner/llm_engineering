KENYAN LAND LAW - KEY STATUTES AND CASE LAW

THE LAND ACT (NO. 6 OF 2012)

PART I: PRELIMINARY

Section 2 - Interpretation
"Community land" means land lawfully held, managed and used by a specific community as defined in Article 63 of the Constitution.
"Public land" means land as defined in Article 62 of the Constitution.
"Private land" means land as defined in Article 64 of the Constitution.

THE THREE CATEGORIES OF LAND IN KENYA (Article 61, Constitution)

1. PUBLIC LAND (Article 62)
Includes: land not held privately or communally; forests, water bodies, mineral land; land used for government purposes; land unoccupied and not registered.
Managed by: National Land Commission on behalf of the national and county governments.

2. COMMUNITY LAND (Article 63)
Held in trust by county governments on behalf of communities.
Includes: land lawfully registered in the name of group representatives; land transferred to a specific community and any other land declared to be community land.

3. PRIVATE LAND (Article 64)
Includes: registered land held by any person under freehold tenure; land held by any person under leasehold tenure; and any other land declared private land under an Act of Parliament.

THE LAND REGISTRATION ACT (NO. 3 OF 2012)

Section 24 - Effect of First Registration
On first registration, the proprietor holds the land subject to: any encumbrances noted in the register; and overriding interests that affect the land.

Section 25 - Protection of Proprietor's Title
The rights of a proprietor of land, whether acquired on first registration or subsequently, shall not be liable to be defeated except as provided in this Act; and shall be held by the proprietor, together with all privileges and appurtenances belonging thereto, free from all other interests and claims whatsoever.

Section 26 - Certificate of Title
(1) The certificate of title issued by the Registrar upon registration, or to a purchaser of land upon a transfer or transmission by the proprietor shall be taken by all courts as prima facie evidence that the person named as proprietor of the land is the absolute and indefeasible owner.
(2) The certificate of title shall not be questioned except on grounds of fraud or misrepresentation to which the person is proved to be a party.

ADVERSE POSSESSION IN KENYA

Section 7 - Limitation of Actions Act (Cap 22)
An action may not be brought by any person to recover land after the end of twelve years from the date on which the right of action accrued.

Requirements for Adverse Possession in Kenya:
1. Actual possession of the land
2. Possession must be open, not secret
3. Possession must be peaceful
4. Possession must be continuous for 12 years
5. Possession must be without the owner's consent

Key Case: Wambugu v Njuguna [1983] KLR 172
Court: Court of Appeal
Held: For adverse possession to succeed, the claimant must show actual, open, peaceful, and continuous possession that is adverse to the title holder for the statutory period.

LAND TRANSACTIONS AND CONVEYANCING

Section 38 - Transfer of Land
Land may be transferred by an instrument of transfer in the prescribed form signed by the transferor and the transferee.

Section 39 - Transmission on Death
On the death of a proprietor of land, the land shall be transmitted to the personal representatives of the deceased.

NOTABLE LAND LAW CASES IN KENYA

Case: Nyambura Waweru v Thuka Investments Ltd [2020] eKLR
Court: Environment and Land Court at Nairobi
Facts: Dispute over ownership of land in Nairobi. The plaintiff claimed adverse possession; defendant relied on registered title.
Held: Registration of land under the Land Registration Act creates indefeasible title except in cases of fraud. The plaintiff's claim of adverse possession failed because the Land Registration Act 2012 caps adverse possession claims against registered land.

Case: Peter Gitau v Mary Wanjiku & 2 Others [2019] eKLR
Court: Environment and Land Court
Facts: Dispute over family land following the death of the registered owner.
Held: The court applied the Law of Succession Act to determine beneficial ownership of land. A daughter has equal inheritance rights to land as a son following constitutional equality provisions.

SPOUSAL RIGHTS TO MATRIMONIAL PROPERTY

The Matrimonial Property Act (No. 49 of 2013)

Section 6 - Matrimonial Property
Matrimonial property includes the matrimonial home or homes; household goods and effects in the matrimonial home or homes; and any other immovable and movable property jointly owned and acquired during the subsistence of the marriage.

Section 7 - Ownership of Matrimonial Property
Ownership of matrimonial property vests in the spouses according to the contribution of either spouse towards its acquisition, and shall be divided between the spouses if they divorce or their marriage is otherwise dissolved.

Case: Jane Mwangi v John Mwangi [2018] eKLR
Court: High Court, Family Division
Held: Contributions to matrimonial property are not limited to financial contributions. A spouse who manages the home and raises children while the other earns income makes a significant contribution to the acquisition of family assets.

THE ENVIRONMENT AND LAND COURT

Established under the Environment and Land Court Act (No. 19 of 2011) to handle disputes relating to:
- Land
- Environment
- Use and occupation of land
- Title to land
- Agreements relating to land

Jurisdiction: Original and appellate jurisdiction over land disputes in Kenya.

COMPULSORY ACQUISITION

Article 40(3) - The State may deprive a person of property if: the deprivation results from an acquisition of land or an interest in land or a conversion of an interest in land, or title to land, in accordance with Chapter Five; or the property is held by a person who is not a citizen of Kenya as prescribed by an Act of Parliament.

Section 107 - Land Act - Procedure for Compulsory Acquisition
(1) The national or county government may acquire land compulsorily if the land is required for public purposes or in the public interest.
(2) The owner must be given prompt payment of full and fair compensation.
(3) The owner has the right to challenge the acquisition in court.

Case: Boniface Ndirangu Mwangi v National Land Commission [2021] eKLR
Court: Environment and Land Court
Facts: Petitioner's land acquired for road expansion without adequate notice or compensation.
Held: Compulsory acquisition without due process and fair compensation violates both the Land Act and Articles 40 and 47 (fair administrative action) of the Constitution. The petitioner was entitled to proper compensation including disturbance allowance.
