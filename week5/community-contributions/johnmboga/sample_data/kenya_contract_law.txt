KENYAN CONTRACT LAW - KEY CASES AND PRINCIPLES

THE LAW OF CONTRACT ACT (CAP 23) - KEY PROVISIONS

Section 2 - Application of English Law
The common law of England relating to contract, as modified by the doctrines of equity, shall apply to Kenya insofar as circumstances permit and subject to such qualifications as local circumstances render necessary.

ESSENTIAL ELEMENTS OF A VALID CONTRACT IN KENYA

1. OFFER AND ACCEPTANCE
For a valid contract, there must be a clear offer by one party and an unequivocal acceptance by the other. The acceptance must mirror the offer exactly - any variation constitutes a counter-offer.

Key Case: Carlill v Carbolic Smoke Ball Co [1893] (applied in Kenyan courts)
Principle: An advertisement can constitute a binding offer if it is sufficiently definite and shows intention to be bound. In Kenya, this principle was applied in commercial advertisement disputes.

2. CONSIDERATION
Every simple contract must be supported by valuable consideration. Consideration is something of value given by the promisee in exchange for the promisor's promise.

Key Principle from Kenyan Case Law:
- Consideration must move from the promisee
- Past consideration is not good consideration
- Consideration need not be adequate but must be sufficient

Case: Peter Murimi Kamau v James Mwangi Waruingi [2018] eKLR
Court: High Court of Kenya at Nairobi
Held: The court reaffirmed that a promise to pay a debt already owed constitutes good consideration for a new agreement, provided there is some additional benefit or detriment.

3. INTENTION TO CREATE LEGAL RELATIONS
The parties must intend their agreement to be legally binding.

Domestic Arrangements: Generally presumed NOT to be legally binding.
Commercial Arrangements: Generally presumed TO BE legally binding.

Case: Balfour v Balfour [1919] (applied in Kenya)
Principle: Agreements between spouses in a domestic context do not generally constitute legally binding contracts.

4. CAPACITY
Every party must have capacity to contract. In Kenya:
- Minors (under 18): Limited contractual capacity; contracts are voidable
- Persons of unsound mind: Contracts voidable
- Corporations: Can only contract within their objects clause (ultra vires doctrine)

5. LEGALITY
A contract must not be illegal or contrary to public policy.

Case: Ndegwa v Mwangi [2015] eKLR
Court: Court of Appeal of Kenya
Held: A contract to commit a criminal offence under Kenyan law is void ab initio and no court will enforce it.

BREACH OF CONTRACT UNDER KENYAN LAW

Types of Breach:
1. Actual Breach - failure to perform at the time performance is due
2. Anticipatory Breach - repudiation of contract before performance is due

Remedies Available in Kenyan Courts:
1. Damages - the primary remedy
   - General damages: compensate for losses naturally flowing from breach
   - Special damages: must be pleaded and proved specifically
   - Mitigation: innocent party must take reasonable steps to minimize loss

2. Specific Performance
   - Equitable remedy awarded at court's discretion
   - Commonly awarded for contracts involving unique land or property
   - Not awarded where damages are adequate

3. Injunction
   - Restrains a party from breaching contract
   - Requires urgency and balance of convenience

4. Rescission
   - Available for misrepresentation, mistake, duress, or undue influence

NOTABLE KENYAN CONTRACT LAW CASES

Case: Nairobi Java House Ltd v Nairobi Street Kitchen Ltd [2019] eKLR
Court: High Court of Kenya, Commercial Division
Facts: Dispute over franchise agreement and intellectual property rights
Held: A franchise agreement creates binding obligations; breach of exclusivity clause entitles franchisee to damages.

Case: Kenya Commercial Bank Ltd v Njoroge [2016] eKLR
Court: Court of Appeal
Facts: Bank sought to enforce guarantee agreement against the defendant
Held: A guarantee is a secondary obligation and the guarantor's liability is coextensive with that of the principal debtor. The court must consider whether proper disclosure was made before signing.

Case: Cooperative Bank of Kenya v Patrick Mwangi Njuguna [2020] eKLR
Court: High Court, Banking Division
Principle: Banks owe a duty of care to customers; standard form contracts must be read against the party seeking to rely on exclusion clauses (contra proferentem rule).

MISREPRESENTATION IN KENYAN CONTRACT LAW

Types:
1. Fraudulent Misrepresentation - false statement made knowingly
2. Negligent Misrepresentation - false statement made carelessly
3. Innocent Misrepresentation - false statement made without fault

Remedy: Rescission and/or damages depending on the type

Case: Mukami Gitau v Nairobi City Council [2014] eKLR
Court: High Court of Kenya
Held: A party induced to enter a contract by fraudulent misrepresentation is entitled to rescind the contract and recover all money paid, plus damages for any consequential loss.

STANDARD FORM CONTRACTS AND CONSUMER PROTECTION

The Consumer Protection Act (No. 46 of 2012) provides protections against unfair contract terms.

Key Provisions:
- Unconscionable terms are void
- Consumers may seek rescission of contracts containing unfair terms
- Service providers must disclose all material terms

Case: James Mwai v Barclays Bank of Kenya [2017] eKLR
Court: High Court, Consumer Division
Held: A bank cannot rely on a clause buried in fine print that significantly deprives the consumer of rights they would reasonably expect to have.
