# Pain Management Guide

## Understanding Pain Types

### Acute Pain
- Sudden onset, usually from an identifiable cause (injury, surgery, dental work)
- Typically resolves as the underlying cause heals
- Duration: days to weeks

### Chronic Pain
- Persists for 3+ months, may not have a clear ongoing cause
- Examples: arthritis, fibromyalgia, chronic back pain, neuropathy
- Requires ongoing management strategy

## OTC Pain Relievers Available at CareFirst

### Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
- **Best for:** Headaches, fever, mild-moderate pain, arthritis pain
- **CareFirst Price:** $7.49 (100ct, 500mg Extra Strength)
- **Max dose:** 3,000mg/day (some guidelines say 4,000mg; lower limit is safer)
- **Key warning:** Avoid with alcohol (liver damage risk). Check all other medications — many combo products (cold medicines, sleep aids) contain hidden acetaminophen.
- **Advantage:** Safe for most people, does not irritate the stomach, safe during pregnancy

### Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)
- **Best for:** Inflammation, muscle pain, menstrual cramps, dental pain, sprains
- **CareFirst Price:** $6.99 (50ct, 200mg)
- **Max dose:** 1,200mg/day OTC (higher with prescription)
- **Key warning:** Can irritate stomach, increase bleeding risk, affect kidneys. Take with food.
- **Avoid if:** Stomach ulcers, kidney disease, on blood thinners, third trimester pregnancy

### Naproxen (Aleve)
- **Best for:** Longer-lasting pain relief (8–12 hours), arthritis, menstrual cramps, tendinitis
- **CareFirst Price:** $8.99 (50ct, 220mg)
- **Max dose:** 660mg/day OTC
- **Key warning:** Same NSAID precautions as ibuprofen. Longer duration means fewer daily doses.

### Aspirin
- **Best for:** Headaches, minor pain, fever. Low-dose (81mg) used for heart protection (only as prescribed).
- **CareFirst Price:** $3.99 (120ct, 325mg)
- **Key warning:** Not for children/teens (Reye's syndrome risk). Increases bleeding risk.

## Choosing the Right Pain Reliever

| Situation | Best Choice | Reason |
|-----------|------------|--------|
| Headache | Acetaminophen or ibuprofen | Both effective; acetaminophen if stomach-sensitive |
| Menstrual cramps | Ibuprofen or naproxen | Anti-inflammatory action targets prostaglandins |
| Arthritis (chronic) | Naproxen or Voltaren gel | Longer-lasting; topical option for localized joints |
| Post-workout soreness | Ibuprofen + ice | Reduces inflammation |
| Back pain | Ibuprofen/naproxen + lidocaine patch | Oral + topical combo |
| Toothache | Ibuprofen | Best OTC option for dental pain (see dentist promptly) |
| Fever | Acetaminophen | Effective and gentle |
| On blood thinners | Acetaminophen ONLY | NSAIDs increase bleeding risk |
| Pregnant | Acetaminophen ONLY | NSAIDs unsafe especially in third trimester |

## When to See a Doctor
- Pain persists more than 7–10 days despite OTC treatment
- Pain is severe or worsening
- Pain accompanied by numbness, tingling, or weakness
- Pain after a significant injury (possible fracture)
- Chest pain or abdominal pain (seek emergency care)
- You need pain relief regularly (more than 2–3 times per week)

## CareFirst Pharmacist Tip
Never combine two NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen + naproxen) — this increases side effect risk without better pain relief. You CAN alternate acetaminophen and ibuprofen if single-agent relief is inadequate. Ask a CareFirst pharmacist for a safe alternating schedule. Bring all your medications for a free interaction check.
