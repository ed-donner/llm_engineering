# Diabetes Management Basics

## Understanding Diabetes

### Type 1 Diabetes
- Autoimmune condition — the body's immune system attacks insulin-producing cells
- Requires insulin therapy (injections or insulin pump)
- Usually diagnosed in childhood or young adulthood
- Approximately 5–10% of diabetes cases

### Type 2 Diabetes
- The body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough
- Often linked to lifestyle factors (diet, weight, physical activity) and genetics
- Can often be managed with oral medications, lifestyle changes, and sometimes insulin
- Approximately 90–95% of diabetes cases
- Increasingly diagnosed in younger adults and adolescents

### Prediabetes
- Blood sugar levels higher than normal but not yet diabetic
- A1C between 5.7% and 6.4%
- Fasting glucose between 100 and 125 mg/dL
- Reversible with lifestyle changes
- Without intervention, often progresses to type 2 diabetes within 5–10 years

## Key Numbers to Know
| Test | Normal | Prediabetes | Diabetes |
|------|--------|-------------|----------|
| Fasting blood glucose | Below 100 mg/dL | 100–125 mg/dL | 126+ mg/dL |
| A1C | Below 5.7% | 5.7–6.4% | 6.5%+ |
| Random blood glucose | Below 140 mg/dL | — | 200+ mg/dL (with symptoms) |

**A1C target for most diabetics:** Below 7.0% (individualized — discuss with your doctor)

## Blood Sugar Monitoring Tips
- Check your blood sugar as recommended by your doctor (typically before meals and at bedtime)
- Keep a log — use the CareFirst app, a paper logbook (free at the pharmacy counter), or your meter's built-in memory
- Know your target range (typically 80–130 mg/dL before meals, below 180 mg/dL 2 hours after)
- Recognize hypoglycemia symptoms: shakiness, sweating, confusion, rapid heartbeat — treat immediately with 15g of fast-acting carbs (glucose tablets, juice)
- Recognize hyperglycemia symptoms: increased thirst, frequent urination, blurred vision, fatigue

## When to Seek Medical Attention
- Blood sugar consistently above 300 mg/dL
- Blood sugar below 54 mg/dL (severe hypoglycemia)
- Symptoms of diabetic ketoacidosis: nausea/vomiting, fruity breath, rapid breathing, confusion
- Persistent numbness or tingling in hands/feet (neuropathy)
- Non-healing wounds, especially on feet
- Vision changes

## CareFirst Pharmacist Tip
Proper insulin storage is critical: unopened insulin should be refrigerated (36–46°F). Once in use, most insulin pens can be kept at room temperature for up to 28 days. Never freeze insulin and discard if it appears cloudy (clear insulin) or has particles. CareFirst provides free insulin storage guidelines and travel kits — ask at the pharmacy counter.
